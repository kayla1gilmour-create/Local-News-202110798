How Sustainable is Glasgow 2026? An Evaluation of the Commonwealth Games’ Eco Goals

Commonwealth Games Scotland has selected four of Glasgow’s most coveted venues to host the events of the Commonwealth Games, but how do they align with the city’s ecological aims?

For the second time since 2014, the Commonwealth Games will take place in Glasgow. The Glasgow 2026 Games has scaled back the events to avoid heavy public spending, and plans to use innovative means to make these Games not only economically sustainable, but also climate-conscious. To do this, they have implemented an eight-mile rule that situates the Games’ venues within a set distance of each other.

The Games take place in only four of the city’s venues – Scotstoun Stadium, Sir Chris Hoy Velodrome, Tollcross Swimming Pool and the Scottish Events Campus (SEC). Between the venues, the Games will host ten events such as basketball, athletics and cycling. The Glasgow 2026 Games website says that the public will be able to access all venues by train to minimise greenhouse gas emissions in light of the city’s goal to become net zero by 2030. The locations of these venues will also facilitate an equitable transport system, as it will allow those who don’t drive to easily access the Games.

Glasgow became one of the leading cities in eco-innovation following the events of COP26 in 2021. The climate convention had two main outcomes – the Glasgow Climate Pact and the Paris Rulebook. The Pact focusses on the limitation of greenhouse gas emissions, while the Rulebook wants consistency and honesty in accounts about the emissions. In 2026, the city places amongst the top five in the Global Sustainability Destination (GDS) Index. These developments make Scotland’s emission goals achievable by 2045, the Climate Change Committee’s most recent study shows.

The SEC shows how the Games plan to use existing sites to achieve Glasgow’s sustainable goals. The venue plays an essential role in the sustainability of the Glasgow 2026 Games, and aims to meet goals that will reduce the Games’ greenhouse gas emissions. The venue will focus on waste reduction by minimising single-use plastics and expanding recycling facilities.

Low Emission Zones (LEZ) have made an undeniable impact on air pollution in the city. Levels of greenhouse gas emissions have fallen by 34% within Glasgow’s Low Emission Zone in the city centre. Not only that, but outside of the zone, emissions have fallen by 22%. Despite this, scientific findings indicate that Glasgow can only lessen emissions by 60% if backed by £23.5bn of investment. The COVID-19 pandemic saw the lowest volume of greenhouse gas emissions in existing data, but since then, the volume has steadily risen. This can be seen in the following chart:

This is mostly the consequence of transport use, Transport Scotland says. In 2023, transport accounted for 33% of greenhouse gas emissions in the country. Car usage fell steeply due to the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic but has since risen sharply. Consequently, the Glasgow 2026 Games aim to tackle this by adopting the eight-mile rule for its venues.

Renovations of Scotstoun Stadium may complicate Glasgow’s ecological and sustainable aims. The venue will host the Games’ athletic events, such as pole vault, shot put and long jump. To accommodate these events, the stadium will receive a new ‘world class’ 400m track. The demolition of Scotstoun Stadium may release toxic emissions into the air, impacting local air quality. Demolition can cause dust disposition and visible dust plumes, says London City Hall.

Despite this, Commonwealth Games Scotland believe the benefits to the public will outweigh ecological costs. They vow that the new stadium will be given to the public after the events, allowing the city access to another ‘world class facility.’

The Chief Executive of Glasgow 2026, Phil Batty, said: ‘We can’t wait to see Scotstoun Stadium come alive in 2026.’

Glasgow’s ambitions to eliminate greenhouse gas emissions can be seen in the eight-mile venue rule and waste reduction goals, yet renovations at Scotstoun Stadium complicate these aims.

The Rise of Walking and Fall of Cycling in Glasgow

The volume of cyclists is falling in Glasgow as the amount of walking rises, Glasgow’s Walking and Cycling Index finds.

The Index shows that walking and wheeling remain the most common ways to commute in the city. In 2025, 93% of people said they walk or wheel, and 57% walked or wheeled at least five times each week. This value has risen by 1% since 2023 and 3% since 2021. The most common motive to walk has been for ‘destination.’ That includes shopping, commuting to a place of employment and going to school. Cycling saw the opposite effect, with only 5% of Glaswegians cycling at least five times each week in 2025, 2% less than in 2023.

Cycling Scotland’s website shows the volume of bikes on Glasgow’s cycling routes in 2025. The routes that saw the most cyclists include South City Way beside Govan, and East City Way by Daldowie Road. East City Way will soon see the addition of new signals, footways and cyclist detection technology so people can safely walk and cycle in the city. Glasgow City Council continues to advocate for walking by implementing schemes that intend to make walking and wheeling active, safe and inclusive in the city. On top of this, May signals ‘National Walking Month,’ that dedicates 31 days to the ‘power of walking.’ This initiative, alongside the Glasgow Kiltwalk and the Glasgow Walking App signify some of the city’s attempts to advance walking in the city.

Despite the impacts of these initiatives, in 2025, 58% of people with disabilities felt unsafe walking and wheeling locally, as the quality of pavements made it difficult to navigate the city. The Avenues initiative has been implemented to handle these accessibility issues. The scheme involves building inclusive public spaces, including walking and cycling routes. While people don’t always feel safe walking in the city, walking is believed to be significantly safer than cycling. The Walking and Cycling Index says cycling is believed to be one of the least safe ways to commute, as only 45% of people feel safe cycling locally – 25% less than walking. In 2018, ‘Bike Life,’ based on the Copenhagen Bicycle Account, compiled cycling statistics in Glasgow to assess city cycling development, the impact of cycling and new initiatives. While Glasgow has 182 miles of cycle routes, Bike Life finds that most remain unknown to the public.

Nonetheless, Cycling Scotland’s findings point to a positive outlook for cycling in the city. They show the places that see the most and the least cyclists at all times of day, and have found that cycling peaks at weekday commuting times. Glasgow continues to motivate people to cycle in the city. The Cycle Hire Scheme that came to Glasgow in 2014 has seen massive success. It was initially run by Nextbike, but was taken over by Voi Technology in 2025. This new scheme makes a minimum of 1000 e-bikes available 24/7 at over 100 locations in Glasgow, Glasgow City Council’s website states. The change to e-bikes reflect the city’s sustainability goals. The Walking and Cycling Index indicates that walking, wheeling and cycling saves 30000 tonnes of greenhouse gas emissions. 22% of Glaswegians want to drive less, but don’t know about the options available to them.

Based in Glasgow, Sunny Cycles aims to make cycling accessible to people of all ages and levels of ability. They help anxious cyclists gain confidence on busy roads and junctions. 69-year-old Joe Dolan holds sessions with Sunny Cycles, and he believes cycling in Glasgow is ‘getting better.’ He says the success of Sunny Cycles was unexpected, and they want people to ‘keep coming and stay active.’

He says people think cycling is an unsafe way to commute because of ‘hostility’ shown to cyclists. ‘If you cycle often you become immune to it, but one incident could put you off for good,’ he says.

If Glasgow wants cycling to rise again, it must build not only bike lanes, but also public confidence. Until then, walking remains the most accessible way to commute in the city.
