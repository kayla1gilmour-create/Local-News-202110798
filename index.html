How Sustainable is Glasgow 2026? An Evaluation of the Commonwealth Games’ Eco Goals

Glasgow 2026 has set out to be the most climate conscious Games yet, but how do the venues align with the city’s ecological aims?

For the second time since 2014, the Commonwealth Games will take place in Glasgow. The Glasgow 2026 Games, that will run between 23 July and 2 August, has scaled back the events to avoid heavy public spending, and plans to use innovative means to make these Games not only economically sustainable, but also climate conscious. To meet these goals, Glasgow 2026 is following new sustainability guidelines, such as the eight-mile rule between venues and waste reduction schemes.

Glasgow became one of the leading cities in eco-innovation following the events of COP26 in 2021. The key outcome was the Glasgow Climate Pact that focusses on reducing greenhouse gas emissions. While Scotland aims to climate emissions completely by 2045, Glasgow wants to achieve it by 2030. In 2026, the city places amongst the top five in the Global Sustainability Destination (GDS) Index. These developments make Scotland’s emission goals achievable by 2045, the Climate Change Committee’s most recent study shows.

Low Emission Zones (LEZ) have made an undeniable impact on air pollution in the city. Emission levels have fallen by 34% within Glasgow’s Low Emission Zone in the city centre. Not only that, but outside of the zone, emissions have fallen by 22%. Despite this, scientific findings indicate that Glasgow can only lessen emissions by 60% if backed by £23.5bn of investment. Transport Scotland’s Publication has shown that volume of greenhouse gas emissions in Glasgow can mostly be linked to the use of cars and taxis. The study shows that transport was the cause for 33% of Scotland’s emissions in 2023. The onset of COVID-19 saw the usage of cars fall massively, but it shot back up after lockdown. Consequently, the Glasgow 2026 Games aim to tackle this by adopting the eight-mile rule. The Games take place in four of the city’s venues – Scotstoun Stadium, the Sir Chris Hoy Velodrome, Tollcross Swimming Pool and the Scottish Events Campus (SEC). Between the venues, the Games will host ten events such as basketball, athletics and cycling. The Glasgow 2026 Games website says that the public will be able to access all venues by train to minimise greenhouse gas emissions in light of the city’s goal eliminate emissions by 2030.

The eight-mile rule will also facilitate an equitable transport system, as it allows those who cannot drive to easily access the venues by public transport. The map below shows the locations of the Commonwealth Games venues:

The SEC shows how the Games plan to use existing sites to achieve Glasgow’s sustainable goals. The venue plays an essential role in the sustainability of the Glasgow 2026 Games, and aims to meet goals that will reduce the Games’ greenhouse gas emissions. The venue will focus on waste reduction by minimising single-use plastics and expanding recycling facilities. The SEC was the host of COP26, and continues to build on the successes of the climate event. Between 2021 and 2023, it began to follow waste-efficiency initiatives such as the ‘Stack Cup’ initiative, that involves paying a deposit of £1 for a cup that the venue refunds if the cup is given back at the end of an event. Since the beginning of the initiative, the use of 2.01 million single-use plastic cups has been avoided.

Renovations of Scotstoun Stadium may complicate Glasgow’s ecological and sustainable aims. The venue will host the Games’ athletic events, such as pole vault and shot put. To accommodate these events, the stadium will receive a new track in an ‘investment by Glasgow 2026.’ The installation of the track began in June 2025, and the second phase is set to begin this month.

‘We’re expecting some of the world’s best athletes to join us in Glasgow,’ Commonwealth Games Scotland says, ‘the replacement of the track – which was laid in 2010 when the stadium was built – will mean the installation of a new, world-class track.’

Demolition of this scale can vitally impact local air quality, says London City Hall. While Commonwealth Games Scotland believes that the new track is essential for hosting elite-level events, the demolition of the old stadium and installation of the new stadium impede Glasgow’s ecological aims.

Despite this, Commonwealth Games Scotland believe that the benefits to the public will outweigh any ecological costs. They vow to give the new stadium back to the public after the Games, and allow the city access to a new ‘world class’ facility. ‘We can’t wait to see Scotstoun Stadium come alive in 2026,’ Phil Batty, the Chief Executive of Glasgow 2026, says.

With Glasgow 2026 now in sight, the balance between the city’s ambitions for the event and goals to limit emissions has inevitably come into question. The coming months will show if Glasgow can meet the demands of the Games while upholding the city’s ecological goals.

The Rise of Walking and Fall of Cycling in Glasgow

Glasgow’s commuting habits have been changing, Glasgow’s Walking and Cycling Index finds, as the volume of cyclists is falling and the amount of walking rises.

The Index shows that walking and wheeling remain the most common ways to commute in the city. In 2025, 93% of people said they walk or wheel, and 57% walked or wheeled at least five times each week. This value has risen by 1% since 2023 and 3% since 2021. The most common motive to walk has been for ‘destination.’ That includes shopping, commuting to a place of employment and going to school. Cycling saw the opposite effect, with only 5% of Glaswegians cycling at least five times each week in 2025, 2% less than in 2023.

Cycling Scotland’s website shows the volume of bikes on Glasgow’s cycling routes in 2025. The routes that saw the most cyclists include South City Way beside Govan, and East City Way by Daldowie Road. East City Way will soon see the addition of new signals, footways and cyclist detection technology so people can safely walk and cycle in the city. Glasgow City Council continues to advocate for walking by implementing schemes that intend to make walking and wheeling active, safe and inclusive in the city. On top of this, May signals ‘National Walking Month,’ that dedicates 31 days to the ‘power of walking.’ This initiative, alongside the Glasgow Kiltwalk and the Glasgow Walking App signify some of the city’s attempts to advance walking in the city.

Despite the impacts of these initiatives, in 2025, 58% of people with disabilities felt unsafe walking and wheeling locally, as the quality of pavements made it difficult to navigate the city. The Avenues initiative has been implemented to handle these accessibility issues. The scheme involves building inclusive public spaces, including walking and cycling routes. The pilot for the scheme was Sauchiehall Avenue, that has gained enhanced, widened pavements, cycling space and intelligent lighting. The changes made to the space can be seen in the following:

While people don’t always feel safe walking in the city, walking is believed to be significantly safer than cycling. The Walking and Cycling Index says cycling is believed to be one of the least safe ways to commute, as only 45% of people feel safe cycling locally – 25% less than walking. In 2018, ‘Bike Life,’ based on the Copenhagen Bicycle Account, compiled cycling statistics in Glasgow to assess city cycling development, the impact of cycling and new initiatives. While Glasgow has 182 miles of cycle routes, Bike Life finds that most remain unknown to the public. Cyclists claim that better visibility and sustained investment could help shift public confidence.

Nonetheless, Cycling Scotland’s findings point to a positive outlook for cycling in the city. They show the places that see the most and the least cyclists at all times of day, and have found that cycling peaks at weekday commuting times. The fact that cycling rises at these times suggests that people have been choosing bikes to make routine trips and not only for fitness. Glasgow continues to motivate people to cycle in the city. The Cycle Hire Scheme that came to Glasgow in 2014 has seen massive success. It was initially run by Nextbike, but was taken over by Voi Technology in 2025. This new scheme makes a minimum of 1000 e-bikes available 24/7 at over 100 locations in Glasgow, Glasgow City Council’s website states. The change to e-bikes reflect the city’s sustainability goals. The Walking and Cycling Index indicates that walking, wheeling and cycling saves 30000 tonnes of greenhouse gas emissions. 22% of Glaswegians want to drive less, but don’t know about the options available to them.

Based in Glasgow, Sunny Cycles aims to make cycling accessible to people of all ages and levels of ability. They help anxious cyclists gain confidence on busy roads and junctions. 69-year-old Joe Dolan holds sessions with Sunny Cycles, and he believes cycling in Glasgow has been ‘getting better.’

Joe says that people think cycling is an unsafe way to commute because of ‘hostility’ shown to cyclists. ‘If you cycle often you become immune to it, but one incident could put you off for good,’ he says. He hopes that Sunny Cycles can continue to 'keep people coming and staying active.'

If Glasgow wants cycling to rise again, it must build not only bike lanes, but also public confidence. Until then, walking remains the most accessible way to commute in the city.
